Variational Inference and application

Jun 16, 2022

Suppose that we have the joint distribution of \( p( x,z)\) where \( x\in \mathbb{R} ^{n}\) is observed data point and \( z\) is latent variable \( z\in \mathbb{R} ^{m}\). For example, we observe \( x\) sequences of text and \( z\) as some topic related to given text or the hidden parameters of the distribution in traditional inference setting. The joint describe the relation between these points. The goal here is to infer the information about latent or hidden \( z\) by observing the variable \( x\). In general, we want the posterior \( p( z|x)\) where can be expressed using Baye's Theorem. So this is somehow relate to bayesian inference. We relate posterior to prior as \begin{equation} p( z|x) =\frac{p( z) p( x|z)}{\int p( z) p( x|z) dz} . \end{equation}where the normalizing constant ensure that the posterior \( p( z|x)\) sum up to one and is called marginal distribution \( p( x)\). This can can also be view as probabilistic graphical model where there exist direct pointing from hidden variable to observable variables, a conditional \( p( x|z)\). In given example, the posterior is \( p( topic|data)\) which we want to infer from observed text. It involve integration over entire latent variable \( z\) dimension, I abuse a little of notation here for simplicity. Instead of finding the true posterior \( p( z|x)\), we use surrogate function as approximation: \begin{equation} q( z) \approx p( z|x) \end{equation} intuitively we want to find 'the best' \( q\) that as close as possible to the true distribution. This can be view as minimizing the matrix which describe dissimilarity of two distributions, the KL-divergence between \( q\) and the \( p\): \begin{equation} q^{*} =\underset{q\in Q}{\mathbf{argmin}} \ D_{KL}[ q( z) \| p( z|x)] \end{equation} where now I introduce again a useless formula that cannot be computed directly because of the posterior \( p( z|x)\). Note that we do not optimize over set of variables but the function of \( q\) over possible set of \( Q\) as the name suggested 'variational'. And the possible set \( Q\) here is a tractable family of distributions. To explicitly see why it is useless, write down the objective function explicitly as \begin{equation} D_{KL} =\mathbb{E}_{z\sim p( z)}\left[\log\frac{p( z)}{p( z|x)}\right] =\int _{z_{1}} \cdot \cdot \cdot \int _{z_{D}}\log\frac{p( z)}{p( z|x)} dz_{0} \cdot \cdot \cdot dz_{D} \end{equation} The objective involve the posterior which is unknown in general and integrating this is catastrophic. Now if working around this equation will come across evidence lower bound: \begin{equation} D_{KL}[ q( z) \| q( z|D)] =-\mathbb{E}_{z\sim q( z)}\left[\log\frac{p( z,D)}{p( z)}\right] +\log p( D) =-L( q) +\log p( D) \end{equation}The last term is called evidence and is always nonpositive (log of value between 0 and 1) and is independent on surrogate, it is depend only on observed data point which is also fix. The term \( L\) must be positive since kl-divergence is non-negative and is less than the evidence \( \log p( x) \geqslant ELBO\), thus the name ELBO, \( L( q)\). This bound can be found be using Jensen's inequality and is also the same way for deriving EM algorithm. If it is equal to the evidence, \( -L=\log p( D)\), the kl-divergence shrink to zero too. Write down ELBO as \begin{gather} ELBO( Q) \ =\ \mathbb{E}[\log \ p( z,\ x)] \ −\ \mathbb{E}[\log q( z)]\\ =\mathbb{E}[\log \ p( x|z)] +\ \mathbb{E}[\log p( z) \ −\ \log \ q( z)]\\ =\ E[\log \ p( x|z)] \ −\ D_{KL}( Q( Z) \| P( Z)) \end{gather} ELBO is then a expected log-likelihood of observed data minus kl-divergence between true dribution and surrogate one, and can be think like trade off between each other. We then can state the optimization problem in equation (3) as minizing the lower bound evidence (ELBO): \begin{equation} q^{*} =\underset{q\in Q}{\mathbf{argmin}} \ L( q) \end{equation} We ignore the evidence \( \log p( D)\) is not depend on \( q\). This can be stated also as maximize the ELBO is quivalent to minimizing KL-divergence. Note that the ELBO is not convex. We can simply optimizing problem by consider mean field VI where it is assume that the each of latent variable \( z\) is mutually independent and its functional distribution is a distinct factors, \( Z=\{z_{i}\}\). Suppose there are \( m\) latent spaces and it is also not the model of the observed data: \begin{equation} q( Z) =\prod _{i=1}^{m} q_{i}( Z_{i}) \end{equation}We can compute expectation of over ELBO and take follow the gradient with respect to the variational parameters. But this in general won't work well in many desired model that do not assume the conjugacy. Instead of finding expectation over variational distribution, we take first the derivative and find the expectation: \begin{gather} \nabla _{v} ELBO( v) =\nabla _{v}\mathbb{E}_{q( z)}[\log \ p( z,\ x) -\log q( z)]\\ =\mathbb{E}_{q( z;v)}[ \nabla _{v}\log \ q( z;v) \nabla _{v}(\log p( x,z) -\log q( z;v))]\\ \approx \frac{1}{N}\sum _{n=1}^{N}[ \nabla _{v}\log \ q( z_{n} ;v) \nabla _{v}(\log p( x,z_{n}) -\log q( z_{n} ;v))] \end{gather} Once we obtained the expectation of gradient and that also know the distribution which gradient sampled from, we can perform Monte Carlo approximation where sampled from variational distribution. For example, variational autoencoder (VAE) is such stochastic VI algorithm where the encoder can be interpreted as posterior \( p( z|x)\). It encode some hidden information or latent variable in full vector \( x\in \Re ^{n}\) to \( z\in \Re ^{m}\) where \( n >m\). This encode approximate the posterior for selected dimension that represent latent space. And the decoder \( p( x|z)\) is generative model where it decode information on latent space to distribution of actual data \( x\). VAE optimize over posterior and generative distribution by follow the gradient with respect to variational and generative parameters: \begin{equation} f=\mathbb{E}_{z\sim q( z)}[\log \ p( x|z)] -D_{KL}[ q( z|x) \| p( z)] \end{equation} The first term is reconstruction loss (mapping from latent space to \( x\)) and second term is interpreted as regularized term.
